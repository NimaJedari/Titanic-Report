Section 1

Data cleaning & imputation

The raw dataset contained missing values across three variables. The following cleaning decisions were applied before modeling to ensure a complete, well-structured dataset of 891 rows.

Age — mean imputation
177 missing values (20%) filled with the column mean (29.7 years). Preserves all rows for training.
Cabin — binary flag
687 missing values (77%). Converted to Has_Cabin (1/0) — a proxy for wealth and deck access.
Embarked — mode imputation
2 missing values filled with Southampton (S), the most frequent embarkation port (72% of passengers).
Fare — log transformation
Heavy right skew with extreme outliers (max $512). Applied log1p transformation to normalise the distribution.
SibSp & Parch — kept as-is
~10 extreme values per variable identified but retained — large families are valid real-world observations.
Dropped columns
Name, Ticket and PassengerId removed — no predictive value for survival modeling.
Section 2

Exploratory data analysis

Prior to modeling, each predictor was examined for its individual relationship with the survival outcome.

Feature importance ranking

A Random Forest model ranked all predictors by predictive contribution. Age, Sex and log Fare dominated at roughly equal importance (~0.25 each).

Survival rate by categorical predictors

Sex showed the largest gap (74.2% female vs 18.9% male). Passenger class dropped from 63% in 1st to 24.2% in 3rd. Cherbourg passengers survived at 55.4% vs 33.9% for Southampton.

Survival by age group

Young passengers (0–18) survived at 50.4% — nearly double mid age (36.3%) and older (34.4%) groups, confirming child prioritization during evacuation.

Log Fare distribution by survival

Survivors paid higher fares — median log Fare ~3.3 vs ~2.5 for non-survivors, validating Fare as a complementary predictor alongside Pclass.

Section 4

Key findings & conclusion

Sex was the strongest predictor. Females survived at 74.2% vs 18.9% for males, reflecting the "women and children first" evacuation policy.
Age group showed a clear pattern — young passengers (0–18) survived at 50.4%, nearly double older groups, confirming child prioritization.
Passenger class showed a steep gradient — 63% in 1st class dropping to 24.2% in 3rd, driven by deck proximity and social priority.
Fare (log transformed) ranked as a top-3 predictor, capturing wealth effects beyond Pclass alone.
Data cleaning was decisive — mean imputation and log transformation pushed accuracy from 78.2% to 81.6%, a bigger gain than any model change.
Logistic Regression outperformed Random Forest across all configurations, consistent with the small dataset size where simpler models generalise better.

The final model achieves 81.6% accuracy on held-out test data, with F1-scores of 0.85 for non-survivors and 0.76 for survivors.
